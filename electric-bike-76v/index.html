Genesis

The First Hardware Build

Before writing simulators or engineering complex software architectures, you have to understand the physical realities of power delivery. This 76V electric bike was my first complete hardware build.

The objective was not aesthetic perfection—it was raw functional engineering. I focused entirely on high-current routing, structural mounting, and integrating a custom battery architecture with a high-performance VESC motor controller to push a 3kW BLDC hub motor safely.

Context

Why EVO vHIL Exists

Building this physical 76V system was the catalyst for the rest of my engineering portfolio. Debugging control logic, tuning throttle curves, and managing BMS states on a live, physical machine capable of pulling massive current is inherently risky and tedious.

The Engineering Pivot

My upcoming project is a custom 300V EV architecture with a bespoke schematic and PCB design. Knowing the dangers of live debugging from this 76V build, I realized I needed a simulator. The entire EVO vHIL framework was built so I can validate the firmware for my 300V architecture before it ever touches physical voltage.

Hardware

System Architecture

The powertrain and energy storage were engineered to provide high continuous discharge with significant thermal overhead.

  • →
    Energy Storage: A custom 76V, 75Ah battery pack built using a 24S5P configuration of 33140 cylindrical LiFePO4 cells (15Ah each).
  • →
    Battery Management: A JK BMS handles active balancing and safety cutoffs for the 24-series cell topology.
  • →
    Powertrain: A 3kW brushless DC (BLDC) hub motor integrated directly into the rear wheel.
  • →
    Motor Controller: A VESC rated for 100V and 200A, providing massive headroom for the 76V system and allowing fine-tuned control over phase currents and regenerative braking.
